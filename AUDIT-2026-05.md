# Content audit against Google May 2026 AEO/SEO guidance

*Date: 2026-05-16 · Site: joynguyenwrites.com · Articles audited: 195*
*Guidance source: developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/ai-optimization-guide*

## TL;DR

The site's **technical** SEO/AEO is excellent (BreadcrumbList, Article schema, llms.txt, llms-full.txt, Person sameAs, valid sitemap — all in place from the prior pass). The risk is **content**, specifically against Google's new "non-commodity content" and "scaled content abuse" guidance:

1. **All 195 articles share a fixed boilerplate tail** ("Joy's editorial perspective", "How I would use this before booking", "Traveler questions this answers", "Trust check for this transport advice", plus a meta-commentary paragraph). This looks like AI-generated template residue, not first-hand writing.
2. **A meta-commentary line — "This first-person layer is editorial, not pretend diary writing…" — leaked into 195 of 195 published articles.** This should never be visible to readers.
3. **129 articles contain a "Trust check for this transport advice" section even when the article is not about transport** (e.g. Hoi An service quality, Mekong Delta ecotourism, cultural travel).
4. **60 review articles (30 Peru + 30 Vietnam) follow an identical 8-H2 template** with near-identical phrasing — exactly the "thin variant pages" pattern Google's scaled-content-abuse policy targets.
5. **Word counts are uniformly 639–843 words (median 679)** — narrow range typical of template-generated content rather than topic-driven writing.

These are the kind of signals AI synthesizers (and Google's helpful-content systems) will increasingly downrank. Fixing #1 and #2 is the highest-leverage cleanup; #3–#5 are larger content workstreams.

---

## Methodology

1. **Heuristic scorer** (`/tmp/jn_audit_score.py`) processed all 195 articles, extracting per-article metrics: word count, H2 count, citation count from Article JSON-LD, first-person markers, commodity title patterns, eyebrow-cluster size, and specific-data signals (dollar amounts, percentages, distances, dates). Risk score 0–100, weighted against Google's four themes.
2. **CSV output:** `/tmp/jn_audit_scores.csv` — ranked list, all 195 rows.
3. **Score distribution:** 9 articles at 0, 110 at 5, 5 at 15–20, 69 at 25, 2 at 40.
4. **Deep-read sample:** 5 articles — the 2 highest-risk research articles, 1 representative review-template article, 1 score-0 research article, and 1 score-0 transport article — to validate scoring and find patterns the scorer missed.
5. **Boilerplate detection:** verbatim phrase grep across all 195 articles.

## Critical findings

### Finding 1 — Meta-commentary leaked into production (195/195 articles)

Every article ends with this paragraph:

> "This first-person layer is editorial, not pretend diary writing. I use it to show how I would interpret the evidence, what I would verify, and where I would slow a traveler down. That keeps the article useful for people and answer engines without claiming personal use of every operator, route, or research setting."

This reads as a *note about the writing*, not content for readers. It's the kind of disclaimer the writer or LLM would put in an internal draft and remove before publishing. For Google's helpful-content systems and AI synthesizers, this is a strong signal of templated/AI-generated content.

**Fix:** Strip this paragraph from all 195 articles. One-line script.

---

### Finding 2 — Universal boilerplate sections (195/195)

Every article has the same closing structure:

- `<h2>Joy's editorial perspective</h2>` — paragraph beginning "My editorial read is that …"
- `<h2>How I would use this before booking</h2>` — generic decision-filter paragraph
- `<h2>Traveler questions this answers</h2>` with 3 FAQ Q&A pairs
- Meta paragraph (finding #1)
- `<h2>Trust check for this transport advice</h2>` (in 129/195, including non-transport articles)

These sections aren't bad in principle — adding editorial perspective and FAQs is fine. But they are **identical phrasing across 195 articles** and often **don't match the article topic**.

| Phrase | Article count | Example mismatches |
|---|---|---|
| "Joy's editorial perspective" | 195 | — (universal) |
| "How I would use this before booking" | 195 | — (universal) |
| "Traveler questions this answers" | 195 | — (universal) |
| "Trust check for this transport advice" | **129** | Appears in articles about food, ecotourism, culture, service quality |
| "Transport advice becomes trustworthy" | 129 | Same — verbatim paragraph in 129 articles |
| "The original thought here is simple" | 129 | Identical sentence in 129 articles |

**Fix options:**
- **Quick win:** Drop the "Trust check for this transport advice" section from the 129 non-transport articles. Keep it on the ~40 articles about transport/buses/routes where it genuinely fits, and rewrite to be specific to each route.
- **Medium effort:** Rewrite "Joy's editorial perspective" sections per-article so the paragraph names a specific instinct, route, or operator detail Joy has thought about. The current "My editorial read is that [quick-answer repeated]" template adds no information.
- **Higher effort:** Rewrite the FAQs to be article-specific — current FAQ Qs are sometimes about "food experience" on service-quality pages, or "transport" on cultural-travel pages.

### Finding 3 — Topic-mismatched boilerplate (concrete examples)

I deep-read `articles/hoi-an-service-quality-research.html` (about Hoi An service quality, with `<p class="eyebrow">Hoi An satisfaction research</p>`). It contains:

- "**Joy's editorial perspective**: My editorial read is that **food travel** works best when it connects appetite with place..." (food, not service quality)
- "**Traveler questions this answers**": Q&A about "food experience", "food tour", "before booking food"
- "**Trust check for this transport advice**" section in an article about Hoi An tailors and hotels

`articles/mekong-delta-ecotourism-research.html` has the same transport boilerplate and a "Joy's editorial perspective" that says *"in Peru and Vietnam those details shape the whole day"* — Peru reference in a Vietnam-only article.

**This is the strongest signal of AI-generated scaled content** in the audit. Google's scaled-content-abuse policy explicitly calls out content "primarily to manipulate rankings or generative AI responses".

---

### Finding 4 — Review-template clones: 60 articles (30 + 30)

The Peru (`Peru travel company review`) and Vietnam (`Vietnam travel company review`) review clusters each have 30 articles following the same 8-H2 template:

1. Best for
2. What Tripadvisor shows
3. What Google review signals add
4. What travelers seem to praise
5. Watch-outs before you book
6. Joy's verdict
7. Booking questions to ask
8. How to use reviews wisely
9. Joy's editorial perspective (universal boilerplate)
10. How I would use this before booking (universal boilerplate)
11. Traveler questions this answers (universal boilerplate)

Each review opens with the same disclaimer paragraph in all 60:

> "This is a review synthesis, not a sponsored recommendation and not a claim of personal use. I read the public review signals travelers normally check before booking..."

Whether this is "scaled content abuse" depends on whether each review surfaces genuinely different operator information. Spot-checking `peru-hop-review.html` vs `ab-expeditions-review.html`: the operator-specific paragraphs (Tripadvisor numbers, Google review themes, named routes) are different, but the framing and 6+ closing sections are interchangeable.

**Risk:** Google's AI now matches relevance without exact keyword overlap, so 60 individual review pages compete with each other and may be consolidated into 2 hub pages with deeper per-operator detail. The hub pages already exist — and the per-operator pages are arguably variants of those hubs.

**Recommendation:** Don't delete. Instead:
- **Trim the universal boilerplate tail** (finding 1 + 2 fixes apply here too).
- **Add genuinely operator-specific content** — current Tripadvisor scrape signals are not enough. Add: route Joy has personally seen the operator on, pickup point clarity, named guides if known, recent (2026) reviewer quotes, comparison to 1-2 named competitors. ~150 unique words per article.

### Finding 5 — 11 commodity-style titles

```
hoi-an-service-quality-research      How to choose ...
mekong-delta-ecotourism-research     How to choose ...
hanoi-cultural-heritage-tourism-quality   How to ...
con-dao-destination-image-loyalty    How to ...
[+7 others]
```

Google's example of commodity vs non-commodity:
- Commodity: "7 Tips for First-Time Homebuyers"
- Non-commodity: "Why We Waived the Inspection & Saved Money: A Look Inside the Sewer Line"

Joy-equivalent rewrites:
- "How to choose Mekong Delta ecotourism well" → "Why one night in the Mekong Delta beats every day-trip I've taken"
- "How to choose Hoi An service quality" → "The tailoring shop in Hoi An that taught me what 'service quality' really means"

These are still researchable, but lead with a *concrete experience or claim*. (Joy may not have those specific anecdotes — but the title alone signals POV.)

### Finding 6 — 7 articles with zero citations in JSON-LD

```
cusco-altitude-sickness-research.html
raymi-llaqta-cultural-travel.html
what-travelers-want-peru.html
ha-long-bay-satisfaction-research.html
hanoi-revisit-intention-research.html
hoi-an-service-quality-research.html
mekong-delta-ecotourism-research.html
```

Some have a "Research used" link in the body text (e.g. SSRN papers) but it's not surfaced in `Article.citation[]` JSON-LD. AI engines that read structured data won't pick up the source attribution.

**Fix:** One-line script — for each, extract the `<a href>` inside the `.source-box` and add to the `citation` array in JSON-LD.

---

### Finding 7 — Word-count uniformity

All 195 articles fall in the 639–843 range (median 679). A site of genuine human writing would show wider variance — short notes (300 words) and deep guides (1,500–3,000). This narrow band is a template fingerprint that AI systems and human auditors notice.

**No quick fix** — depth comes from real writing, not padding. But the next ~50 articles Joy publishes should aim for genuine length variation: 250-word "quick answer" pages where useful, 2,000-word guides where the topic deserves it.

## Action plan, tiered

### Tier 1 — Same-day script work (high impact, ~30 min)

| # | Action | Articles touched | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Remove the "This first-person layer is editorial..." paragraph | 195 | Removes most damaging meta-commentary leak |
| 2 | Remove "Trust check for this transport advice" + its 2 paragraphs from non-transport articles | ~89 (129 minus ~40 genuine transport articles) | Eliminates the most jarring topic mismatches |
| 3 | Add the existing source-box URL to `Article.citation[]` JSON-LD for the 7 zero-citation articles | 7 | Restores AEO attribution surface |

### Tier 2 — Surgical content rewrites (multi-day, judgement-required)

| # | Action | Articles touched | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | Rewrite "Joy's editorial perspective" paragraph per article so each one names a specific detail/route/observation | 195 (or ~135 if scoped) | Removes the "[quick answer repeated]" pattern; adds genuine POV |
| 5 | Rewrite the 3 FAQs per article so they match the actual article topic | 195 | Fixes food-FAQs-on-service-quality-articles type issues |
| 6 | Trim 11 commodity titles to lead-with-claim phrasing | 11 | Better CTR + first impression for AI synthesis |
| 7 | Add 100–150 words of genuinely operator-specific content to each of the 60 review articles | 60 | Reduces scaled-content risk on the largest cluster |

### Tier 3 — Editorial direction going forward

- **Aim for word-count variance:** future articles should range 250–2,500 words based on what the topic actually deserves.
- **One distinct lived-experience hook per new article:** a specific named guide, exact route timing, weather day, food stall, breakdown story, etc. This is the durable competitive moat per Google's new guidance.
- **Don't fan out keyword variations:** "Lima to Huacachina by bus" + "Lima to Huacachina direct vs combo" + "Lima Paracas Huacachina chain" + "Huacachina dune buggy bus timing" — currently 4+ articles on overlapping queries. Consider whether these should be one definitive guide rather than four near-duplicates.
- **Use the answer-box + headings + citations structure that already works on the score-0 articles** (e.g. `peru-public-bus-safety-research.html`, `paracas-huacachina-nazca-buffers.html`) — these read as genuinely useful even where they share the boilerplate tail.
